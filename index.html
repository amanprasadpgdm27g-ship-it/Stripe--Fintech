Section 01

The Friction — getting paid was harder than building the product

Before Stripe, a developer who wanted to charge a customer online had to open a business bank account, apply for a merchant account through an acquiring bank, get approved by a card network, integrate a clunky payment gateway, and handle PCI compliance — a process that could take weeks and required paperwork most solo founders had never seen before.

The people who felt it most were exactly the people trying to start something small: indie developers, side-project founders, and early-stage startups. They could stand up a working product in a weekend, then lose a month untangling how to actually collect money for it.

Key idea — Paul Graham later called this exact pattern "schlep blindness": the problem was big and valuable, but so unglamorous that almost nobody with the skills to fix it ever tried.
Section 02

The Founders — two brothers who kept hitting the same wall

Patrick and John Collison, brothers from the small village of Dromineer, County Tipperary, Ireland, were both self-taught programmers from childhood. As teenagers they built and sold an eBay-tools startup, Auctomatic, through Y Combinator for roughly $5 million — Patrick was 19, John was 17.

That exit didn't scratch the itch. Patrick went to MIT, John to Harvard, and both kept building side projects online — running into the same payments wall every single time. In late 2009 they took a month-long trip to Buenos Aires and built the first prototype under a Unix-style working name.

Section 03

The Business Model — a small cut of money that was already moving

Stripe's core model is a per-transaction fee — the standard published rate is 2.9% + 30¢ on a card-not-present US transaction, paid by the business accepting the payment, not the customer. Stripe keeps a small net slice after passing most of that fee through to card networks and issuing banks.

Payments
Per-transaction processing fee, paid by the merchant on every successful charge.
Radar
Fraud detection priced per screened transaction (a few cents each), paid by the merchant.
Billing & Revenue suite
Subscription and invoicing tools, priced as a percentage of recurring revenue managed.
Atlas
Company incorporation for founders — a flat setup fee plus a small annual fee.

The pattern underneath all of it: Stripe doesn't charge for access to a market, it charges a toll on money that was already going to move — so revenue scales automatically with how much commerce its customers do.

Section 04

Geography & Scale — from Palo Alto to 1.6% of world GDP

Stripe incorporated in Palo Alto, California in 2010, went through Y Combinator, and launched publicly in the US in September 2011 after a private beta. Expansion since then has been steady rather than explosive — new countries added a few at a time, prioritizing depth of local payment methods (iDEAL in the Netherlands, Boleto in Brazil, Alipay in China) over speed.

Section 05

Status Today — still private, still growing, still not in a hurry

Independent & operating

Stripe remains privately held. In February 2026 it ran a tender offer — a share sale that gives current and former employees liquidity without a public listing — that valued the company at $159 billion, its highest mark ever.

Rather than pivoting, Stripe has been layering new bets onto the same core: acquisitions in stablecoin infrastructure (Bridge, Privy) and a purpose-built payments blockchain called Tempo, alongside tools for "agentic commerce" that let AI agents initiate payments on a person's behalf.

What this tells us — a profitable infrastructure company that moves money for a living can afford to keep optionality rather than answer to public markets on a quarterly clock.
